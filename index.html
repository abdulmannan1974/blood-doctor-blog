01
Paradigm Shift

The Bone Marrow vs Lung Debate Is a False Dichotomy

For over a century, haematologists have argued about where platelets are born: bone marrow or lung? The 2024 landmark study by Asquith et al. in Blood used flow cytometry, light-sheet fluorescence microscopy (LSFM), two-photon intravital imaging, AND single-cell RNA sequencing to conclusively demonstrate that the bone marrow harbours 11-fold more MK progenitors and 75-fold more LT-HSCs than the lung.

Yet Lefrancais & Looney's 2019 review in Physiology compiled evidence that the lung contributes 50-98% of final platelet release, with direct intravital visualization showing platelet shedding in pulmonary capillaries.

The synthesis no single paper states:

These findings aren't contradictory — they describe two phases of a single pipeline. The bone marrow is the manufacturing site (megakaryopoiesis), while the lung is the finishing line (terminal platelet shedding from circulating MK fragments). Neither organ alone completes thrombopoiesis. The platelet is born in the marrow but delivered in the lung.

Asquith et al. (2024) Blood 143(3):272-278 • Lefrancais & Looney (2019) Physiology 34:392-401

02
Bone Marrow

Textbook Proplatelets Are an In Vitro Artefact — In Vivo, Megakaryocytes Use "Mass Membrane Fusion"

Every haematology textbook shows elegant thin proplatelet extensions budding off megakaryocytes. Brown et al. (2018) in Life Science Alliance used intravital correlative light-electron microscopy (CLEM) of living mouse bone marrow and discovered that >90% of MK projections entering sinusoids are large protrusions (>3 μm), not the fine proplatelet extensions (2-3 μm) seen in culture.

The mechanism is completely different:

Large protrusions use mass membrane fusion between the internal membrane system (IMS) and plasma membrane, not dynein-dependent microtubule sliding. Microtubule content at the leading edge is only 14% (vs 42% in proplatelets). The scission point is in the marrow parenchyma, not at the vessel wall — meaning shear forces alone do not explain platelet release.

Once detached from the MK body, 72% of these free large protrusions convert to proplatelet morphology, suggesting a two-step process: first extrusion as large protrusions, then remodeling into proplatelets in the circulation.

Brown et al. (2018) Life Sci Alliance 1(2):e201800061

03
Bone Marrow

Megakaryocytes Drill Through Endothelium Using "Podosome Collectives" — Borrowed From Osteoclasts

How does a 50-100 μm megakaryocyte squeeze its cytoplasmic processes through a continuous endothelial barrier without tearing it? Eckly et al. (2020) in JTH discovered the answer: MKs form in vivo podosome-like structures — F-actin pillars oriented perpendicular to the endothelium, interconnected by a contractile actomyosin network.

These podosomes work collectively: groups of closely positioned structures cooperate to create transcellular pores through endothelial cells. Loss of Arp2/3 complex profoundly impairs pore initiation; loss of myosin IIA scatters pore distribution.

Clinical connection — Wiskott-Aldrich Syndrome:

In WAS, mutations in the WAS protein lead to defective actin polymerization → impaired podosome formation → defective MK intravasation → ectopic platelet release within the marrow → microthrombocytopenia. This study provides the mechanistic explanation for the small, dysfunctional platelets in WAS.

Eckly et al. (2020) J Thromb Haemost 18:2987-3001

04
Lung

The Lung Contains TWO Completely Different MK Populations With Opposite Functions

Gelon, Fromont & Lefrancais (2022) in Frontiers in Immunology crystallized a distinction that redefines what a megakaryocyte IS:

Out-of-the-box implication:

The megakaryocyte is not a single cell type with one function. It's a bifunctional cell class — haemostatic in the marrow and vasculature, immunological in the lung interstitium. This is analogous to how macrophages have tissue-specific identities (Kupffer cells, microglia, alveolar macrophages). Are lung-resident MKs the "megakaryocyte equivalent" of tissue-resident macrophages?

Gelon et al. (2022) Front Immunol 13:1029223

05
Developmental Biology

Six Transcriptionally Distinct MK Subpopulations Exist — Including a CD14+ "Immune MK"

Wang et al. (2021) in Cell Stem Cell performed scRNA-seq on MKs from human yolk sac (4 WPC) and fetal liver (8 WPC) and identified six MK subpopulations with distinct transcriptional programs:

MK1 (glycolysis), MK2 (cell cycle), MK3 (thrombopoiesis/coagulation), MK4 (niche support — secretes IGFBP3 and PTN to maintain HSCs), MK5 (translational initiation), and MK6 (immune functions — expresses C1QC, CTSS, CYBB, MAFB, SAMHD1).

The CD14+ immune megakaryocyte:

MK6 cells co-express CD42b+ and CD14+ and show enrichment of innate immune response and macrophage activation pathways. They can be generated from hESCs in vitro. Morphologically, CD41a+CD42b+CD14+ cells resemble monocyte-macrophages more than classical MKs — raising the question: is this a megakaryocyte behaving like a macrophage, or a macrophage wearing megakaryocyte markers?

Crucially, MK subpopulations followed two distinct developmental trajectories: Direction 1 (thrombopoiesis-biased, expressing PF4, GATA1, NFE2) and Direction 2 (immune-biased, expressing IGFBP3, COL1A1, CTSS, CYBB). This bifurcation appears to be an ancient evolutionary feature, present from the earliest stages of human embryonic development.

Wang et al. (2021) Cell Stem Cell 28:535-549

06
Neonatal

Fetal Megakaryocytes Are Not "Immature" — They're a Parallel Operating System

Davenport, Liu & Sola-Visner (2022) in Blood overturned the assumption that fetal/neonatal MKs are simply immature adult cells. Despite being small and low-ploidy (78% are ≤8N vs 33% in adults), cord blood MKs have all ultrastructural features of maturity: abundant α-granules, von Willebrand factor, P-selectin, and a well-developed demarcation membrane system.

The molecular master switch is IGF2BP3, a fetal-specific RNA-binding protein that stabilizes the 7SK snRNA complex, keeping P-TEFb (the transcriptional elongation kinase) inactive. This dampens megakaryocyte morphogenesis genes while allowing proliferation — a developmental strategy that favours many small platelet-producing cells over fewer large ones to rapidly populate expanding fetal blood volume.

Why this matters at the bedside:

Neonatal platelets have low P-selectin → reduced neutrophil binding → different inflammatory responses. TPO/c-MPL axis functions differently at different developmental stages. Platelet transfusion thresholds derived from adult studies may not be physiologically appropriate for neonates.

Davenport et al. (2022) Blood 139(22):3233-3244

07

The Lung Is the World's Most Efficient Platelet Bioreactor — And We Can Exploit It

Fuentes et al. (2010, J Clin Invest) showed that infusing ex vivo-generated mature MKs into mice produced a ~100-fold increase in recipient platelet count vs prior in vitro methods. Wang et al. (2015, Blood) confirmed that human MKs infused into NSG mice release platelets intrapulmonarily that are indistinguishable from donor platelets in size, surface markers, and ability to incorporate into thrombi.

The "cautionary tale" from Wang et al. is critical: ex vivo-produced platelet-like particles (EV-PLPs) are 85% CD41-negative, mostly preactivated, and rapidly cleared by macrophages. But IV-platelets released from intrapulmonary-entrapped MKs are nearly identical to donor platelets. The lung's unique combination of shear stress, turbulence, oxygen tension, and vWF-rich endothelium cannot be replicated in any bioreactor.

The implication: megakaryocyte transfusion, not platelet transfusion, may be the future.

08
COVID-19 / Cardiac

Cardiac Megakaryocytes in COVID-19 Were a Non-Specific Inflammatory Marker — Not a Disease Signature

The early COVID literature sensationalized cardiac megakaryocytes as a disease-specific "immunothrombotic" signature. Gawelek et al. (2022, Histopathology) examined cardiac tissue from 36 COVID-19 and 12 pre-pandemic autopsies. Cardiac MKs were found in 64% of COVID-19 cases but also 40% of pre-pandemic controls with overlapping pathologies (thromboembolic disease, DAD, heart failure).

What the data actually show:

The density was higher in COVID-19 (1.77 vs 0.84 MK/cm²), but this reflects the intensity of inflammation and thromboembolic burden, not a COVID-specific mechanism. Combined with Brill & Halpern's 1948 autopsy data (MKs found in 100% of lungs, 62% of spleens, 36% of kidneys, 13% of hearts), cardiac MKs are a general marker of systemic inflammation that has existed for at least 75 years before COVID-19.

Gawelek et al. (2022) Histopathology 81:600-624 • Brill & Halpern (1948) Blood 3(3):286-291

09
Classification Crisis

CD41 Alone Cannot Define a Megakaryocyte — The Field Has an Identity Problem

Asquith et al. (2024) showed that in murine scRNA-seq data, only ~2% of CD41+ lung cells expressed canonical MK genes (Itga2b, Gp1ba, Gata1, Pf4). The remaining CD41+ cells expressed progenitor and immune-cell markers. In human lung single-cell atlases, only 11 of 60,993 cells (~0.02%) were identified as MKs, and none expressed canonical MK markers.

Combined with Wang et al.'s (2021) discovery of CD14+ immune MKs and Gelon et al.'s (2022) MKL population with immune transcriptional signatures, the field faces a fundamental question: what defines a megakaryocyte?

The identity crisis:

Using CD41 alone massively overestimates lung MK numbers. Using canonical MK genes alone misses the immune subpopulation. The estimates of lung platelet production (7% to 98%) are wildly variable partly because different studies are counting different cell populations. Until the field agrees on a consensus surface marker panel that distinguishes platelet-producing MKs from immune-function MKs from CD41+ non-MKs, the "where do platelets come from" debate cannot be resolved.

Asquith et al. (2024) Blood 143(3):272-278 • Wang et al. (2021) Cell Stem Cell 28:535-549

10
Cardiothoracic Surgery

Post-CPB Thrombocytopenia Is Not Just Consumption — It's Loss of the Lung Production Line

Griffin et al. (2020, Ann Thorac Surg) showed that post-CPB thrombocytopenia (occurring in 26% of 1,364 patients) is independently associated with mortality (OR 6.19), infection (OR 2.08), stroke (OR 3.18), and AKI (OR 1.64). The classical explanation is consumption on the extracorporeal circuit.

The hidden mechanism:

If the lung contributes 50-98% of terminal platelet release (Lefrancais & Looney 2019), and MKcirc increase 3-fold in patients whose lungs are bypassed (Gelon et al. 2022), then CPB thrombocytopenia has a dual mechanism: consumption of platelets on the circuit AND loss of the lung finishing line for new platelet production. The MK fragments that would normally be processed into platelets in the pulmonary vasculature are being shunted past the lung, emerging as unprocessed fragments that are cleared by the reticuloendothelial system. This explains why the thrombocytopenia can be disproportionate to circuit-induced consumption.

Griffin et al. (2020) Ann Thorac Surg 110:50-57 • Lefrancais & Looney (2019) Physiology 34:392-401 • Gelon et al. (2022) Front Immunol 13:1029223

Out-of-the-Box Applications

Where these insights could take us — from bedside to biotech

🩸

Megakaryocyte Transfusion Instead of Platelet Transfusion

Infuse ex vivo-generated MKs and let the patient's own lungs do the platelet production. Each MK generates 100-200 platelets in vivo vs 1-2% yield in vitro. Could solve the 5-day shelf-life problem, cold chain logistics, and bacterial contamination risk of stored platelets.

Near-term feasibility
🫁

Lung-Protective Strategies to Preserve Platelet Production in ARDS

If the lung produces most platelets, then lung-protective ventilation strategies aren't just about gas exchange — they're about preserving the platelet production line. Thrombocytopenia in ARDS may be partly a production deficit, not just consumption. Could reframe the platelet transfusion trigger in critical care.

Near-term feasibility
💊

S1PR Modulators (Fingolimod) as Accidental Thrombopoiesis Disruptors

The S1P/S1PR axis guides proplatelet elongation into the bloodstream (Gelon et al. 2022). Patients on fingolimod (S1PR modulator for MS) develop lymphopenia — but its effects on thrombopoiesis via disrupted S1P-guided MK egress have never been systematically studied. A low-hanging pharmacovigilance fruit.

Near-term feasibility
🧬

IGF2BP3 as a Therapeutic Target for Neonatal Thrombocytopenia

If IGF2BP3 is the molecular switch that keeps fetal MKs in "high-proliferation, low-ploidy" mode (Davenport et al. 2022), then modulating it could shift the balance. Knockdown of IGF2BP3 in neonatal MKs enhances P-TEFb activation, producing adult-like MK features — potentially a precision therapy for severe neonatal thrombocytopenia.

Medium-term
🏭

Biomimetic Lung-on-Chip Platelet Bioreactors

Current bioreactors fail because they lack the lung's unique combination: shear stress (0.4-3.0 Pa), turbulence at bifurcations, high O2 tension driving ROS-dependent cytoskeleton remodeling, and vWF-rich endothelial surfaces enabling GPIb-mediated mechanotransduction. A bioreactor that replicates ALL four features could achieve physiological platelet yields for the first time.

Medium-term
🎯

Platelet "Postcode" Hypothesis: Site of Production Determines Function

Megakaryocytes sort mRNAs non-randomly into platelets. If the lung environment (pathogens, O2, inflammatory signals) imprints different mRNA/miRNA profiles into lung-produced vs marrow-produced platelets, then platelets from different sites might have different functional specializations. Could explain altered platelet reactivity in lung diseases (COPD, asthma, CF).

Long-term / Speculative
🔬

Lung-Resident MKs as Immune Sentinels — A New Drug Target

MKL cells express innate immunity genes (pathogen recognition, inflammation) and sit in the interstitium where they are among the first cells to encounter inhaled pathogens. If they function as immune sentinels, modulating their activity could be a novel approach to pulmonary infections — or conversely, their inappropriate activation could contribute to inflammatory lung disease.

Long-term / Speculative
🫀

Reimagining Cardiopulmonary Bypass: Lung-Inclusive Perfusion

If bypassing the lung causes platelet production deficits beyond simple consumption, then perfusion strategies that maintain at least partial lung flow during CPB could preserve in vivo platelet production. This could be studied as a modifiable factor in the 26% of post-CPB patients who develop thrombocytopenia — a population with 4.6x higher mortality.

Near-term feasibility

From Aschoff's Drawings to Single-Cell Atlases

In 1893, Ludwig Aschoff at Strasbourg observed "giant-nucleated cells" plugging pulmonary capillaries during infections and hand-drew their forms in Virchow's Archiv. He saw their irregular shapes — 14 to 92 μm long, wedged into 5 μm capillaries — and wondered if they were pathological emboli. 130 years later, we know those cells were megakaryocytes caught in the act of doing exactly what they were designed to do: releasing platelets in the lung.

What Aschoff saw with his microscope and carmine stain, we now quantify with 10X Genomics, CITE-seq, and intravital two-photon microscopy. The cells haven't changed. Only our ability to understand them has.

The platelet is not born in one place. It is conceived in the bone marrow, shaped by its transit through the vasculature, and delivered in the lung. Its siblings may carry different functional "postcodes" depending on where in this journey they are released. And a subset of their parent cells has abandoned platelet production entirely to become immune sentinels in the lung interstitium.

The platelet odyssey continues. The next chapter will be written by those who see the whole distributed system, not just the bone marrow.
